Overview

Intelligent household robots hold the promise of significantly enhancing our quality of life by taking over mundane household chores, allowing us to focus on more productive or leisurely activities. However, the current state of home robotics is limited by single-purpose models or policies that lack the flexibility to adapt to the dynamic environments found in our homes. These rigid models are trained to perform specific tasks and fail to adjust to changes or learn from ongoing experiences, rendering them less effective in real-world scenarios. To address these challenges, it is crucial to develop home robots capable of lifelong learning. Lifelong learning enables robots to build complex, nuanced representations of their surroundings, facilitating continuous adaptation and informed decision-making based on the current state of their environment. This approach allows robots to transcend the limitations of their initial training and evolve through interactions and experiences.

This workshop will explore the development of intelligent home robots that can quickly adapt to new environments by learning user preferences, understanding environmental constraints, and mastering new tasks through demonstrations, interaction and natural language communication. These lifelong learning processes should involve user demonstrations or dialogues, where the robot can ask clarifying questions and receive guidance to resolve ambiguities.

Key questions we would like answered in this workshop include:

  • How can robots effectively learn from human demonstrations and feedback in a natural and intuitive manner?
  • What techniques are needed to facilitate seamless human-robot collaboration in learning and task execution?
  • How can robots learn and adapt to the individual preferences and routines of different household members?
  • What mechanisms are needed to ensure personalized and user-specific interactions and task performances?
  • What representations can serve as robust and flexible memory systems that allow household robots to recall past experiences and apply this knowledge to new situations?
  • What are the optimal strategies for balancing short-term (e.g., ‘working memory’) and long-term memory in robotic systems?
  • How can robots autonomously detect and correct errors in task execution, with or without human intervention?
  • What frameworks can enable robots to learn from their mistakes and improve over time?
